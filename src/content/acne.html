What Is Acne?

Acne is a common skin condition that affects the hair follicles and oil glands in your skin. It happens when pores become clogged with oil, dead skin cells, and bacteria. Acne is NOT caused by poor hygiene or dirty skin!

What Causes Acne?

Acne develops from four main factors working together:

  1. Excess oil production in your skin
  2. Clogged pores from dead skin cells
  3. Bacteria (called C. acnes) that live on the skin
  4. Inflammation (redness and swelling)

** Hormones, genetics, and certain medications can make acne worse.

Skincare Habits

1

Wash your face gently, twice daily

Use a fragrance-free cleanser. Avoid harsh scrubs, astringents, or exfoliating washes, as these can irritate your skin and worsen acne.

2

Use a moisturizer

A fragrance-free, non-comedogenic (won't clog pores) moisturizer helps reduce irritation from acne medications. Apply before and/or after topical medications (sandwich method) to minimize dryness.

3

Choose the right makeup

Look for products labeled "non-comedogenic," "oil-free," or "won't clog pores."

Clean brushes and makeup utensils regularly as bacteria and oils accumulate on these items.

Remove makeup thoroughly before bed as sleeping in makeup can clog pores and worsen acne.

4

Apply sunscreen daily

Use a broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher, especially if you have dark spots from acne. Sun exposure can darken these spots and make them last longer.

5

Avoid touching your face

Hands transfer bacteria and oils to facial skin throughout the day.

!

Don't pick, pop, or squeeze!!

Picking at acne can cause scarring, infection, and worsen dark spots!

Diet Considerations

Research on diet and acne is still evolving, but some studies suggest:

1

Consider a low-glycemic diet

Foods that cause rapid spikes in blood sugar (white bread, sugary drinks, candy, white rice, pastries) may worsen acne. High glycemic foods increase insulin and IGF-1 levels, which stimulate oil production and inflammation. Choosing whole grains, vegetables, and lean proteins may help.

2

Dairy may play a role

Studies show an association between milk consumption and acne. If you notice a connection, consider reducing dairy intake.

3

Omega-3 fatty acids may help

Foods rich in omega-3s (salmon, sardines, walnuts, flaxseed) have anti-inflammatory properties that may benefit skin health.

4

Chocolate

Evidence is mixed; cocoa or high-sugar formulations may worsen acne in some people.

Lifestyle Factors

1

Get regular exercise

Studies show that regular physical activity may indirectly improve acne through metabolic and stress-related effects.

2

Limit prolonged sitting

Sedentary behavior has been associated with acne in some observational studies, though causation is unclear.

3

Prioritize sleep

Going to bed late and poor sleep quality have been linked to acne. Aim for consistent sleep.

4

Change/wash pillowcases regularly

Oils, bacteria, and dead skin cells accumulate on pillowcases.

5

Clean your phone screen, hats, and glasses

Objects that touch your face frequently can transfer bacteria and oils.

6

Manage stress

Stress can worsen acne through hormonal changes.

Important Reminder — Understand Your Treatment!

  • Results take time — be consistent for at least 2–3 months before evaluating improvement.
  • Everyone's skin is different – what works for one person may not work for another.
  • Use sunscreen daily (SPF 30+)
  • ALWAYS SPOT TEST NEW PRODUCTS! Avoid using multiple new products at once!
  • DO NOT PICK AT ACNE! AVOID TOUCHING THE FACE!
